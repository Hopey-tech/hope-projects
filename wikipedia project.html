The Moon

Natural satellite of Earth

Diameter: 3,474.8 km

Orbit: Around Earth

First human landing: 1969 (Apollo 11)

Introduction

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the fifth largest moon in the solar system. It is the brightest and most prominent object in our night sky. The Moon orbits Earth at an average distance of about 384,400 km (238,855 miles) and influences tides, biological cycles, and folklore across cultures.

History

The Moon has fascinated humans for thousands of years. Ancient civilizations used the lunar phases to create calendars. In modern times, the Moon became a target of scientific study and space exploration. The most notable event was the first crewed Moon landing by NASA's Apollo 11 mission in 1969, when Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walked on the lunar surface.

Since then, multiple missions have been sent to study the Moon, including orbiters, landers, and future plans for permanent human presence through programs like NASA's Artemis mission.

Scientific Significance

Scientists believe the Moon was formed around 4.5 billion years ago, possibly from debris left over after a Mars-sized object collided with Earth. Studying the Moon provides insight into early solar system history and planetary evolution.
